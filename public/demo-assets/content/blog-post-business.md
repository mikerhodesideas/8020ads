# 5 Kitchen Renovations That Pay For Themselves at Resale

After fifteen years of renovating kitchens across Melbourne and the Mornington Peninsula, I've had the same conversation hundreds of times: "Will I get my money back if I sell?"

The honest answer is that it depends entirely on what you renovate. Some upgrades return 150% of what you spend. Others, no matter how beautiful they look on Instagram, return thirty cents on the dollar.

Here are the five kitchen renovations that consistently add more value than they cost, based on what I've seen sell in this market over the past decade.

## 1. Stone Benchtops (Replacing Laminate)

**Typical cost:** $4,500 - $8,000 for an average kitchen
**Typical value added:** $8,000 - $15,000

Nothing signals "updated kitchen" to a buyer faster than stone benchtops. And the gap between what laminate says ("this kitchen was done in 2007") and what stone says ("this kitchen was done recently") is worth more than the material difference alone.

Engineered stone (Caesarstone, Essastone, Smartstone) runs $450-$800 per square metre installed. Natural stone is $600-$1,200. For most resale situations, engineered stone gives you the best return because buyers can't tell the difference in photos, and most first inspections are now online.

One thing I tell every customer: go with a 40mm edge, not 20mm. The thicker edge costs an extra $300-$500 across a typical kitchen, but it looks substantially more premium. Every real estate agent I've spoken to says the same thing. It photographs better.

Don't bother with exotic stone for resale. Calacatta-look engineered stone at $550/sqm delivers the same buyer reaction as $1,400/sqm imported marble, and it won't stain when someone leaves a lemon on it overnight.

## 2. Soft-Close Cabinetry (Full Replacement or Reface)

**Full replacement cost:** $12,000 - $22,000
**Reface only cost:** $5,000 - $9,000
**Typical value added:** $15,000 - $30,000

Cabinetry makes up roughly 40% of a kitchen's visual impact. Old timber-grain doors with brass handles date a kitchen more than anything else. Replacing them with flat-panel or shaker-style doors in a neutral finish (white, light grey, or natural timber look) transforms the entire room.

Soft-close hinges and drawer runners are non-negotiable now. Buyers under 45 expect them. The hardware adds roughly $800-$1,200 to a full kitchen's cabinetry cost, and the absence of it is noticed more than its presence. Nobody tours a home and says "oh, the drawers close softly, how lovely." But they absolutely notice when a drawer slams.

If your existing cabinet boxes are structurally sound, refacing (new doors and drawer fronts on existing boxes) is the highest-ROI play in kitchen renovation. You get 80% of the visual impact for 40% of the cost.

We did a refacing job in Frankston last year: $7,200 for new shaker doors, soft-close hardware, and brushed brass handles. The vendor agent told the owners it added at least $20,000 to their sale price. I don't know the exact final number, but they sold above the range.

## 3. Splashback Upgrade (Tiles or Glass)

**Typical cost:** $1,500 - $4,000
**Typical value added:** $3,000 - $7,000

The splashback sits right at eye level. It's one of the first things buyers notice, and one of the cheapest things to change. Replacing a tired tile splashback with large-format subway tiles, natural stone-look porcelain, or coloured glass can make a $15,000 kitchen look like a $30,000 kitchen.

The sweet spot for resale is 300x600mm white or light grey subway tiles. They cost $35-$60 per square metre for the tiles themselves, and installation runs $80-$120 per square metre. A typical kitchen splashback is 3-5 square metres, so you're looking at $1,500-$2,500 all in.

Avoid anything too bold for resale. That emerald green glass splashback might be your dream, but it's someone else's nightmare. Stick with neutrals. You want the buyer to imagine their own life in the kitchen, not admire your taste.

One exception: if the rest of the kitchen is already neutral (white cabinetry, stone benchtops), a single feature panel behind the cooktop in a contrasting material can work well. We've had good results with natural stone offcuts from the benchtop installed vertically as a feature splashback. It ties the kitchen together and costs almost nothing extra if you're already ordering stone.

## 4. Appliance Package (Oven, Cooktop, Dishwasher)

**Typical cost:** $3,500 - $7,000 for the package
**Typical value added:** $5,000 - $10,000

Buyers want stainless steel, and they want brands they recognise. That's the beginning and the end of it. A matching set of mid-range appliances (think Bosch, Westinghouse, or Fisher & Paykel) signals a modern, functional kitchen.

You don't need the top of the range. A $2,200 Bosch oven performs just as well as a $5,500 Miele for resale purposes. Buyers aren't checking spec sheets. They're checking that the appliances look new and match each other.

The three appliances that matter most for resale value:

1. **Oven:** Must be built-in, stainless steel, and made in the last 5 years. Budget $900-$1,800.
2. **Cooktop:** Induction is now preferred over gas in most Melbourne suburbs. The energy rating argument has won. Budget $600-$1,200.
3. **Dishwasher:** Must be integrated or semi-integrated. Freestanding dishwashers make a kitchen look unfinished. Budget $800-$1,400.

Skip the wine fridge, the built-in coffee machine, and the steam oven unless you're renovating a property valued above $2 million. Below that price point, they add cost without proportional value.

## 5. Lighting (Downlights and Under-Cabinet LEDs)

**Typical cost:** $800 - $2,000
**Typical value added:** $2,000 - $5,000

This is the one most people overlook, and it's the one with the highest percentage return. Good lighting makes every other element of your kitchen look better. Bad lighting makes a $40,000 renovation look like a $15,000 one.

Replace any fluorescent tubes or single central pendants with recessed LED downlights. The standard is now warm white (3000K) LEDs on a dimmer. Installation costs $80-$120 per downlight, and a typical kitchen needs 4-6.

Under-cabinet LED strip lighting costs $200-$400 installed and does more for the feeling of a kitchen than almost anything else. It eliminates shadows on the benchtop, makes the splashback glow, and photographs exceptionally well. Real estate photographers love it because it adds depth to kitchen photos.

For evening inspections (which are increasingly common), good lighting is the difference between "this kitchen feels warm and inviting" and "I can't really see the benchtops properly."

## The Hidden ROI: Energy-Efficient Appliances

Here's one that doesn't show up in the resale maths but matters to buyers more every year: energy efficiency.

An induction cooktop uses roughly 30% less energy than a gas equivalent. A modern dishwasher uses 40% less water than one from 2015. LED downlights cost about $15 per year to run versus $80 for halogens.

Buyers are asking about energy ratings now. When a kitchen has a 5-star rated dishwasher, induction cooking, and LED lighting throughout, it signals that the whole house has been thoughtfully updated. It's a proxy for "the previous owners cared about the details."

Our most recent data from customer surveys: 34% of buyers specifically mentioned energy efficiency as a factor in their kitchen assessment. That number was 11% five years ago.

## What to Skip

Three popular renovations that rarely pay for themselves at resale:

1. **Butler's pantries** ($15,000-$30,000). Buyers love them in theory but won't pay the premium. They're eating into your kitchen footprint and the cost-to-value ratio is poor unless the house already has 3+ living areas.

2. **Premium range cookers** ($8,000-$15,000). That La Cornue or AGA looks spectacular, but buyers in the $800K-$1.5M range don't value it proportionally. You'll recover maybe 30% of the cost.

3. **Custom joinery details** ($3,000-$8,000 for pull-out spice racks, appliance garages, etc.). Functional for living in, but invisible in photos and rarely noticed during inspections. Save these for your forever home.

## The Bottom Line

A smart kitchen renovation in the $15,000-$25,000 range, focused on stone benchtops, modern cabinetry, a clean splashback, matching mid-range appliances, and proper lighting, can add $35,000-$65,000 to a property's sale price in the current Melbourne market.

The key is spending money where buyers' eyes go first: benchtops, cabinet fronts, and lighting. Everything else is detail.

If you're renovating to sell, do the five things on this list and stop. If you're renovating to live in, do these five first, then add whatever makes you happy. At least the foundation will be earning its keep when you eventually move on.
